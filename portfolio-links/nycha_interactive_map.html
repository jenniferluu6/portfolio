03 Why It Matters

The Deferred Maintenance Crisis

NYCHA estimates its capital repair backlog at over $40 billion. That number is not an accident. It is a direct consequence of when the portfolio was built. Buildings constructed in the 1950s and 60s are now 60 to 70 years old, and they are reaching the end of their designed service life simultaneously.

Roofs, elevators, boilers, plumbing — the systems that keep 400,000 residents housed — were all installed in the same generation. When they fail, NYCHA has to triage. And with a repair backlog measured in decades, triage is the permanent condition.

"The question is not why NYCHA buildings are in poor condition. The question is why we are surprised."

Federal investment in public housing construction peaked in the 1960s and was effectively withdrawn by the 1970s. The portfolio stopped growing, but the maintenance needs did not.

From Section 9 to Section 8

The PACT program — Permanent Affordability Commitment Together — is NYCHA's attempt to address the backlog by converting public housing units from Section 9 to Section 8 project-based vouchers, bringing in private developers to finance and manage renovations.

I worked on one of these conversions at Fairstead, managing the renovation of Reid Apartments in Brooklyn. Standing inside those buildings every day, I kept coming back to the same question: when were these built? What were they designed for, and what has happened to them since?

This map started as an attempt to answer that question. It became something larger: an argument that the crisis NYCHA faces is not about mismanagement or bureaucracy alone. It is about what happens when a generation of infrastructure ages out all at once, and no one has planned for it.

No matter how efficiently you renovate a building, what determines who can afford to live there afterward is policy.
